Now, on the bigger goal — building real community in a single-family-home suburb, where the default is that everyone disappears into their garage and closes it behind them — a flyer is a good start but it works best as one layer of a few:
1. Personal touch beats paper alone. In a neighborhood where people don't know each other, a flyer from a stranger can read as generic. If you can manage it, walking the flyer to doors yourself (even just 10-15 closest houses) and saying a quick hello does more for turnout than the paper itself. The goal of the event — neighbors meeting neighbors — actually starts at your door.
2. Lower the barrier to "yes." Since you don't know these people well yet, make it easy to commit without over-committing:

Frame it as casual and drop-in ("swing by anytime after 1pm")
BYOB is good — also consider "bring a chair" or "bring nothing, just come" so nobody skips it over not having something to bring
A short RSVP method (text/email on the flyer) helps you gauge headcount for the clam bake without requiring one

3. Multiple touchpoints. People need to see something more than once before it registers:

Flyer/door hanger ~2-3 weeks out
A reminder closer to the date (another door hanger, or if you get anyone's contact info from the first pass, a text blast)
If your neighborhood has a Nextdoor, a local Facebook group, or a physical community bulletin board (mail cluster box areas often have one), post there too

4. Consider a small paper sign-up sheet or QR code on the flyer linking to a simple page with the details, so people can save it/share it with a spouse.
